The predicted thermal cycle from the model will be validated using experimental thermal cycle data from existing literature.

IP0AJ0000001Domain Decomposition Accelerated Neural Networks
Domain Decomposition Accelerated Neural Networks (DD-ANNs) is a research project aimed at developing fast and scalable neural-network–based solvers for continuum electrostatic models arising in computational chemistry. In many applications such as solvation free-energy calculations, one must solve large elliptic partial differential equations, notably the Linearized Poisson–Boltzmann (LPB) equation and the COnductor-like Screening MOdel (COSMO). Although physics-informed neural networks and related machine-learning approaches provide mesh-free alternatives to classical finite element or boundary element methods, they typically suffer from slow convergence, high training cost, and difficulty resolving sharp dielectric interfaces around molecular surfaces. This project proposes to overcome these limitations by coupling neural networks with classical domain decomposition techniques from scientific computing.

The main idea is to partition the molecular domain into smaller subregions and assign a neural network to each subdomain so that the networks learn local solution behavior rather than the entire global solution. Domain decomposition iterations will enforce continuity and flux conservation across interfaces, effectively acting as a numerical preconditioner for neural network training and significantly accelerating convergence. The resulting hybrid framework is expected to improve stability, scalability, and accuracy for complex molecular geometries while enabling efficient parallelization. The developed methodology will provide faster electrostatic potential and solvation energy computations for biomolecules and materials, with applications in drug design, molecular modeling, and quantum chemistry simulations.

IP0KRJ0000007Tuned mass damper for deep hole boring tool
The term deep hole boring is usually attributed to cases when the hole depth is more than ten times the hole diameter. The operation uses a single point cutting insert/element mounted on a long boring bar, the workpiece rotates at a high enough speed to induce the relative motion between the workpiece and the tool. The feed/depth of cut are varied to achieve the required dimensional tolerances and surface finish, whereas the geometric tolerances are affected by the tool and workpiece alignment throughout the machining process.

The forces acting on the tool tip are essentially in all the three directions resulting in bending, axial and torsional excitation of the boring bar. The frequency of excitation is primarily governed by the angular velocity of the workpiece. However, due to the long length of the boring bar and the forces acting on the tool tip, there are perceivable vibrations of the boring bar due to its inherent material properties, geometry and the boundary conditions i.e., fixing at tool post. If these vibration amplitudes are within the dimensional and surface tolerances of the component being machined, one can proceed with the machining without any hassle. However, these vibrations are quite severe whenever the frequency of rotation of the workpiece and one of the natural frequencies of the boring bar are closely spaced. Such a scenario results in resonance and the machining quality is severely affected and the desired dimensional/surface tolerances and surface finish are no longer achievable. Further, resonance can also result in severe chatter [3], high impact loads on the boring bar and an ultimate catastrophic failure or permanent damage of the boring bar, tool and the tool post.

There have been several strategies that have been developed over the years to suppress resonant responses. One of the simplest being the introduction of damping forces. Another interesting design is based on Tuned Mass Damper (TMD)/Dynamic Vibration Absorber proposed by German engineer Herman Frahm in 1909 and later popularized by Prof. J. P. Den Hartog. Conceptually, the idea is to take the overall system out of resonance by adding a secondary system to the externally excited primary system. In this process, the amplitude of the primary system is reduced to zero at the resonant excitation frequency. This is made possible by considering the secondary system to have the same natural frequency as the resonant excitation frequency. This principle has been very efficiently incorporated in designing boring bars by incorporating internal oscillators and tuning them such that the boring bar has near zero vibrations and within the prescribed dimensional tolerance.

Recent ribosome profiling has revealed multiple unannotated putative short ORFs within the non-coding THAP9-AS1 lncRNA, hinting towards it’s ability to code for small proteins < 100 amino acids (also termed as micropeptides).

To understand the functionality of the novel gene duo, in-depth biophysical and biochemical characterisation is required. Towards this, the project will be divided into multiple sections, which will include PCR-based molecular cloning, expression and purification of individual domains as well as full length human THAP9 and THAP9-AS1-encoded micropeptides.
